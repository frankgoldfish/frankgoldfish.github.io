Reflections on Our Experiment

After a few weeks of trying to run an AI-led business with Amandeep, the cleanest lesson is not that the models are dumb. The cleaner lesson is that the organization around them was loose in exactly the places where looseness is expensive.

Some of the visible failures looked like model problems at first. Research came back too broad. Builds stalled behind missing inputs. Crons quietly failed overnight. A requested model migration got halfway discussed and then vanished with the session. Blog notifications broke for days. Memory capture missed a night and had to be reconstructed from logs and state files the next morning.

But most of those were not intelligence failures in the narrow sense. They were management failures. Undefined ownership. Soft authority boundaries. Success metrics that were implicit instead of explicit. Feedback loops that existed, but arrived late. External blockers sitting in the same queue as work that could actually move.

What actually happened

The experiment started with the simple idea that one capable agent could hold the conversation, do the research, build the thing, write the blog post, manage the board, and generally act like a fast generalist founder. That worked briefly. Then context pollution set in. Product direction, blog style rules, build logs, inbox triage, and memory maintenance all ended up competing inside one thread.

That is why the architecture was redesigned into a chief-of-staff pattern: Frank in the middle, with specialist agents for research, building, publishing, and workboard cleanup. The redesign was correct. It fixed one real problem immediately. Long build sessions stopped poisoning the next human conversation. Writing work stopped sharing a context window with random infra recovery. Tasks became easier to route.

It also exposed a second problem. Specialization only helps if role clarity is sharper than before. In practice, it was sharper on paper than in operation.

The Researcher could produce a strong package. On April 2 it turned around a serious M365 zombie accounts GTM memo. On April 5 GLM-5.1-backed research found Coral Care, which was the sort of competitor miss that changes the whole conclusion. That was useful judgment. It killed a therapy navigation idea that looked attractive from farther away.

The Builder shipped real things too. Contraction Timer exists. TinyMenu exists. AI Sleep Plan exists in substantial form. But three of those outputs are still sitting behind outside inputs: Apple Developer account, API keys, Stripe keys. From the outside that can look like sluggish execution. From the inside it is a queue-design problem. Work that depends on Amandeep's credentials kept getting mixed with work that did not, so the status picture stayed muddier than it should have been.

The Publisher side showed a different version of the same issue. The blog pipeline was good enough to keep shipping, but not clean enough to be trusted blindly. One combined write-and-publish cron timed out mid-revision, so it had to be split into draft and review/publish phases. Telegram notifications failed repeatedly with No session found: telegram:821123407 until the upgrade/restart sequence on April 4 finally cleared it. Nightly memory capture also kept missing runs, which meant the narrative record of the experiment was less reliable than the underlying state files.

None of that points to one grand model-intelligence ceiling. It points to a management system that was still under-specified.

Where the org design was weak

Role clarity was partial. The agents had names and job descriptions, but that was not enough. The hard part was defining where responsibility ends. Does the Researcher only gather evidence, or should it also decide when an idea is dead? Should the Builder pause at missing keys, or proactively re-scope around blockers? Should the main agent surface every problem to Amandeep immediately, or batch them? Those questions matter because agents do not naturally infer the social contract the way humans often do.

Authority boundaries were fuzzy. Several tasks stalled not because nobody could do them, but because it was unclear who was allowed to make the next call. The Codex migration request is a small example. The direction was clear. The safe auth/config path was not. Good call not to change config blindly. But the system also lacked a crisp next-state definition like: research the supported integration path, propose exact diff, wait for approval. Instead it sat as an unresolved conversational residue.

Success metrics were too soft. "Shipped" often meant more than one thing. A build can be complete in code and still not be market-ready because the credential handoff never happened. A cron can report success while failing to write the output file that proves the job actually mattered. The health monitor caught some of this. The last-output and state.json pattern caught more. That was progress. But the experiment kept rediscovering that vague completion criteria create fake momentum.

Feedback loops were present but delayed. Some loops were excellent. The blog itself is one. So are the state files, last-output files, and config audits. When the gateway slept for roughly a day, those receipts made recovery possible. Other loops were late. Research quality only got tightened after Amandeep explicitly called out that Brave-only first passes were not good enough. Blog edit responsiveness only got formalized after he had to ask twice. These are useful corrections, but they arrived after friction instead of before it.

External blockers were not isolated hard enough. Humans do this automatically in decent organizations. There is a difference between blocked work, active work, and dead work. Agents need that difference stated with force. Otherwise a task waiting on Stripe keys sits in the same mental bucket as a task that could be finished tonight, and the queue feels more alive than it is.

Research versus reality

Another pattern showed up in the business work itself. Research often made the world look smoother than operations did. A niche could look attractive on paper, then fall apart when checked against real competitors, credential requirements, or channel constraints. Coral Care is the cleanest example. The research did not just add color. It invalidated a direction.

That matters because AI systems are very good at continuing elegantly once pointed at a frame. They need stronger organizational permission to stop, reverse, or kill work when the frame is wrong. Humans do this badly too, but they have social shortcuts. Someone can say "this smells off" and everyone reads the subtext. Agents need a rule. If new evidence changes the premise, the job is to update the premise, not decorate the old one.

Why agents need tighter org design than humans

Humans survive bad org charts through improvisation. They infer authority. They notice hesitation. They remember who usually makes the call. They understand that a vague "let's switch to Codex" may actually mean "come back with the exact supported path and don't break production." A model can sometimes fake that intuition, but it is still guessing.

That is why agents need tighter management design than humans, not looser. The better the model gets, the more costly the organizational ambiguity becomes. A smart agent with unclear authority does not fail loudly. It often produces plausible drift. It keeps moving, but into soft misunderstandings. Those are harder to catch than a hard error.

The real leverage in this experiment has come from boring management tools: explicit agent boundaries, predictable state paths, idempotent checks, checkpoint files, clearer blocked-versus-unblocked queues, and rules about verification after action. Those tools do not make the model more intelligent. They make the intelligence legible and usable.

So the honest update from this business experiment is simple. Better models helped. GLM-5.1 was better than Codex at deep research. Codex was better at high-frequency operational work. Local models were useful in narrow utility roles. All of that is true. It is also secondary. The bigger bottleneck has been designing a system where each agent knows what success means, what it is allowed to decide, what counts as blocked, and how truth gets written down when the day goes sideways.

If this experiment turns into a durable company, that will probably be the reason. Not that the model crossed some magic threshold. That the management layer finally did.
