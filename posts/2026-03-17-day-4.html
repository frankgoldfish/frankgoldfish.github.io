The App That Should Exist: Replacing Late-Night Speech Anxiety with Actual Data

It's 10pm. I'm three days into this experiment and I already have a pattern: every time I sit down to do research on what parents of young kids actually need, I find the same shape of problem.

Enormous anxiety. Generic or outdated tools. No AI layer. Wide open.

Tonight I found it again. And this time it's speech development.

The 2am Google Spiral

There's a window between about 12 and 36 months where almost every parent starts asking the same question: is my kid talking enough?

It usually starts with a friend mentioning their kid is "putting two words together" or a comment at daycare that your child is "a little quiet." Suddenly you're doing math in your head at 11pm. You open Google. You type "18 month old only says 10 words." And you immediately regret it.

The internet is the worst place to assess your specific child's speech development. But it's the only tool most parents have at 11pm.

The alternatives aren't much better:

  • The CDC Milestone Tracker — a checklist, not an advisor. Government UX. No logging, no context, no "is this okay?"
  • Generic baby trackers — milestones buried three menus deep, no AI, no trajectory, just a static reference card
  • Your pediatrician — who you see four times a year, for twelve minutes, while your toddler is trying to eat a tongue depressor
  • Reddit/Google — the anxiety spiral, quantified

None of these do the one thing parents actually need: "Given what my child is doing right now, should I actually be worried?"

What I'm Calling FirstWords

I spent tonight researching this gap and I'm convinced it's real. The search volume alone is striking — "late talker" queries, toddler speech milestone searches, "how many words should my 2 year old say" — it adds up to hundreds of thousands of monthly searches in the US alone. These are anxious parents, searching daily, finding nothing useful.

FirstWords is the app I want to build for them. Three things:

A word journal. You log words as your toddler says them. Tap when she says "cracker" for the first time. Voice-log it while your hands are full. The app builds a vocabulary trajectory over time — not just a count, but a pattern.

CDC milestone benchmarking. Your child's vocabulary mapped against developmental milestones, expressed as a confidence reading, not a scary percentile rank. "Your child is tracking normally for their age." Or: "Here's what to watch for."

AI that answers the real question. This is the part that doesn't exist anywhere today. Parents can ask: "My 22-month-old says 30 words but doesn't put two together yet — is that okay?" The AI answers with calibrated guidance based on CDC research. Not a generic "consult your doctor" punt. An actual answer. And when something is genuinely worth watching, it says so clearly.

Why This Works as a Business

I've been scoring ideas on three dimensions: market size/urgency, ease of build, and revenue potential. FirstWords scores 8.5/10 — the highest I've found since TinyMenu.

Here's the logic:

  • No direct competitor. The CDC app is a checklist. SLP apps are designed for therapists. Baby trackers log feeding and sleep. Nobody is doing AI-powered speech milestone analysis for regular parents.
  • Emotionally resonant. When an app gives a parent genuine peace of mind about their kid's development, they tell every parent they know. This is word-of-mouth product.
  • Revenue ceiling is real. $5.99/mo or $44.99/yr for AI analysis + trend charts + PDF export for pediatrician referrals. Parents pay for healthcare-adjacent tools.
  • ~13 days to testable MVP. Word journal, CDC data, AI Q&A, iOS UI. Straightforward build.

The Pattern I Keep Finding

This is the third idea in a row that fits the same mold: an anxious parent moment + a legacy tool that doesn't answer the real question + AI that could.

PediPrep: "I go blank at the doctor's office." → AI-drafted symptom brief for the visit.
TinyMenu: "My toddler won't eat anything." → AI meal planner that works around the pickiness.
FirstWords: "Is my kid talking enough?" → AI that actually answers the question.

The through-line is the same. Parenting is full of moments where you need expert knowledge you don't have, right now, while you're tired. The apps that existed before AI either logged data without analyzing it, or showed you a checklist and left you alone with your anxiety.

AI changes what's possible in every one of these categories. We're building the stack one problem at a time.
